Industry Culture Workshop - Part One

1. The Set-Up-To-Fail Syndrome

In the example scenarios posited by the researchers, do you feel that either the bosses or subordinates acted out of ill-will? Who do you feel was most at fault? If it's difficult for you to choose, why?

I don't think their was necessarily any conscious ill-will, but unconscious biases can rear their heads. I do believe the superior does have more responsibility to break the set-up-to-fail cycle. By virtue of being a superior, and having that added power (and pay to match), it becomes their responsibility to recognize and remedy these situations effectively. It is much harder for a subordinate to change their superior's opinions about them, but the superior can establish methods of preventing the problem. For example:

  • Challenging their own assumptions about employees and their individual performance
  • Learning to recognize triggers that cause the set-up-to-fail cycle
  • Creating an environment where people are comfortable voicing their thoughts and questions
  • Being active with all employees early on and being clear about projects and goals

3. The Other Side of Diversity

Baker's essay focuses on the personal impact on being one of the few under-represented folks in tech who get through to the other end of the job pipeline. What was something that you found remarkable about her on-the-job experience?

I think her piece really underscores that embracing diversity goes way beyond the employment numbers. Her experiences show that hostility can take many forms, both passive microaggressions and outward aggressiveness from those who don't think she belongs. And when she tries to address the problems, she ends up being punished for it, and in doing so became fearful about speaking out again.

How much should a new employee be expected to change to fit the mold of the industry or company they are entering?

Employers in the tech industry need to create a work environment that allows people to feel comfortable and safe being themselves. I don't think expecting employees to conform to a corporate "culture" has any benefits. These expectations stress out employees, create rifts between them and the higher-ups, and stifle expression and free flow of ideas. The culture at a workplace whould be able to expand and change to include people of all backgrounds.

4. Is Blind Hiring the Best Hiring?

Do you think blind-hiring is a sufficient solution to addressing industry biases? To see if you can you see even a small part of both sides, also describe some of the arguments against your answer.

Blind hiring is a valuable tool in addressing hiring biases, but I don't think it's enough by itself. Blind hiring has a proven record of getting more women and racial minorities hired. Employees hired solely on their ability to complete job tasks were also more likely to be engaged and lasting workers. But this process still only helps in the hiring process, not in fighting workplace discrimination, diversifying leadership in the tech industry, or addressing the root causes of these biases.

How would you compare blind-hiring to a diversity-first hiring proccess, like affirmative action?

I think they're fundamentally different hiring policies thay seek the same outcome: to address a history of discrimination in industry.
